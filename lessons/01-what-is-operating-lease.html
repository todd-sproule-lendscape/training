What is an Operating Lease?

Overview

A lease is a contract that conveys the right to use an underlying asset for a period of time in exchange for consideration. Leases are classified into two main categories from the lessor's perspective:

Operating Lease

The lessor retains substantially all risks and rewards of ownership. The asset remains on the lessor's balance sheet.

  • Lessor recognises the asset
  • Rental income recognised over the lease term
  • Lessor depreciates the asset
Finance Lease

The lease transfers substantially all risks and rewards of ownership to the lessee.

  • Lessor derecognises the asset
  • Recognises a net investment in the lease
  • Finance income recognised over the lease term

Lease Classification Criteria

Under both IFRS 16 and ASC 842, the classification depends on whether the lease transfers substantially all the risks and rewards incidental to ownership. Key indicators of a finance lease include:

  1. Transfer of ownership by end of lease term
  2. Purchase option at a price sufficiently lower than fair value that exercise is reasonably certain (sometimes called a “bargain purchase option”)
  3. Lease term is for the major part of the asset's economic life
  4. Present value of lease payments amounts to substantially all of the fair value
  5. The asset is of a specialised nature that only the lessee can use without major modifications
Key Point: If none of these criteria are met, the lease is classified as an operating lease. The classification is made at the inception date of the lease and is only reassessed if there is a lease modification.

Lessor Accounting for Operating Leases

When a lease is classified as an operating lease, the lessor:

Aspect Treatment
Underlying asset Remains on the lessor's balance sheet
Rental income Recognised on a straight-line basis over the lease term (or another systematic basis if more representative)
Depreciation Asset depreciated from cost to residual value over its useful life
Initial Direct Costs (IDCs) Added to the carrying amount of the asset and expensed over the lease term

RIAD: Rental Income And Depreciation

In Lendscape, the operating lease revenue processing method is known as RIAD — Rental Income And Depreciation. This is the revenue engine that implements straight-line recognition for operating leases, as distinct from the EIR (Effective Interest Rate) method used for finance leases.

Aspect RIAD (Operating Lease) EIR (Finance Lease)
Revenue pattern Straight-line (even distribution) Compound interest (front-loaded)
Interest rate No interest rate calculation XIRR calculation required
Asset ownership Remains with lessor Transfers to lessee
Depreciation Asset depreciation tracked Not tracked by lessor
Primary income Rental income Finance (interest) income
Complexity Lower (proportional allocation) Higher (NPV, IRR, adjustments)
When to use RIAD
  • Operating leases where the lessor retains ownership
  • Even revenue recognition is desired
  • Asset depreciation needs tracking on the lessor's balance sheet
  • Simpler, more transparent accounting is preferred

Key Takeaways

  • An operating lease does not transfer substantially all risks and rewards of ownership
  • The lessor keeps the asset on their balance sheet and depreciates it
  • Rental income is typically recognised on a straight-line basis
  • Both IFRS 16 and ASC 842 govern lessor accounting for operating leases
  • In Lendscape, the operating lease method is called RIAD (Rental Income And Depreciation), distinct from EIR used for finance leases
