What is an Operating Lease?

Overview

A lease is simply a contract where one party (the owner) lets another party use an asset for a set period in return for regular payments. From the lessor's (owner's) perspective, leases fall into two categories:

Operating Lease

Think of it like renting out your car — you still own it, still bear the risk if it breaks down, and still benefit from selling it later. The asset stays on your books.

  • The lessor keeps the asset on their balance sheet
  • Rental income is spread evenly over the lease
  • The lessor records the asset losing value over time (depreciation)
Finance Lease

More like a hire-purchase — the user is effectively buying the asset over time. By the end, ownership has practically shifted to them.

  • The lessor removes the asset from their books
  • Instead, they record what the lessee owes them (a receivable)
  • Interest income is earned over the lease term

How Do You Decide Which Type?

Both IFRS 16 and ASC 842 ask the same basic question: does this lease essentially hand over ownership to the user? If any of these signs are present, it is likely a finance lease:

  1. Ownership transfers to the lessee by the end of the lease
  2. The lessee can buy the asset at a bargain price, so they almost certainly will
  3. The lease covers most of the asset's useful life
  4. The total payments (in today's money) add up to nearly all of the asset's value
  5. The asset is so specialised that only the lessee can realistically use it
Key Point: If none of the above apply, the lease is an operating lease. You decide the classification when the lease starts (the “inception date”), and only revisit it if the lease terms are changed later.

What Does the Lessor Actually Do?

When a lease is classified as an operating lease, the lessor's accounting is relatively simple:

Aspect Treatment
Underlying asset Remains on the lessor's balance sheet
Rental income Spread evenly over the lease term (straight-line), regardless of when cash is actually received
Depreciation The asset loses value over time — this cost is recorded evenly over the asset's useful life
Initial Direct Costs (IDCs) Set-up costs (like broker fees) are added to the asset value and gradually expensed over the lease

RIAD: Rental Income And Depreciation

In Lendscape, operating leases are processed by a method called RIAD — short for Rental Income And Depreciation. It handles the even spreading of income and depreciation. This is different from EIR (Effective Interest Rate), which is used for finance leases and works more like compound interest.

Aspect RIAD (Operating Lease) EIR (Finance Lease)
Revenue pattern Straight-line (even distribution) Compound interest (front-loaded)
Interest rate No interest rate calculation XIRR calculation required
Asset ownership Remains with lessor Transfers to lessee
Depreciation Asset depreciation tracked Not tracked by lessor
Primary income Rental income Finance (interest) income
Complexity Lower (proportional allocation) Higher (NPV, IRR, adjustments)
When to use RIAD
  • Operating leases where the lessor retains ownership
  • Even revenue recognition is desired
  • Asset depreciation needs tracking on the lessor's balance sheet
  • Simpler, more transparent accounting is preferred

Key Takeaways

  • In an operating lease, the owner keeps the asset — they bear the risks and enjoy the rewards
  • The asset stays on the lessor's balance sheet and loses value (depreciates) over time
  • Rental income is spread evenly (straight-line) over the lease, no matter when cash arrives
  • The rules come from IFRS 16 (international) and ASC 842 (US)
  • In Lendscape, the operating lease method is called RIAD (Rental Income And Depreciation), distinct from EIR used for finance leases
